Acute physical injury induces profound and often prolonged systemic metabolic perturbations that extend far beyond the site of injury. Understanding these responses at a pathway level offers the opportunity to improve risk stratification, monitor recovery trajectories, and identify individuals at risk of long-term complications. My research applies targeted and comprehensive metabolic phenotyping to human injury cohorts, with a particular focus on thermal burn injury and traumatic brain injury (TBI).

**Burn Injury**

A consistent finding across my burn injury research is that even non-severe burns — those involving less than 15–20% total body surface area — induce sustained systemic metabolic perturbations that persist well beyond clinical wound closure. Studies in both adults (Journal of Proteome Research, 2024) and children (Scientific Reports, 2022; Burns & Trauma, 2023; Frontiers in Public Health, 2023) have demonstrated prolonged alterations in lipid metabolism, lipoprotein composition, amino acid profiles, and inflammatory markers, with patterns consistent with persistent systemic inflammation and elevated cardiometabolic risk.

Senior-author work published in Burns (2025) demonstrated that plasma lipidomic profiles measured at hospital admission can predict wound re-epithelisation outcomes in non-severe burn patients, supporting the clinical utility of early metabolic profiling for prognosis. Complementary work applying Rapid Evaporative Ionisation Mass Spectrometry (REIMS) to burn wound tissue (Burns, 2022) explored real-time intraoperative tissue classification as a tool to improve surgical decision-making. Ongoing work examines sex-based differences in long-term lipid metabolic responses following paediatric burns, and the neurological consequences of peripheral burn injury in murine models.

**Traumatic Brain Injury**

In traumatic brain injury, co-first-author work published in the Journal of Cerebral Blood Flow and Metabolism (2024) identified a plasma lipid signature in acute human TBI correlated with markers of neuronal injury and neuroinflammation, including neurofilament light chain and interleukin-6. Key discriminating lipid species included lysophosphatidylcholines and docosahexaenoic acid-containing phospholipids, consistent with phospholipase-mediated membrane breakdown following acute neural injury. Parallel work in a rat model demonstrated progressive plasma lipid profile changes with increasing numbers of mild TBI (Metabolites, 2022), supporting the development of blood-based biomarkers for cumulative injury burden.

**Translational Focus**

Across both injury contexts, a central objective is translation — refining metabolic signatures identified in discovery cohorts into targeted panels suitable for prognostic application, including in settings where conventional venous sampling is impractical. This programme is supported by active collaborations with Professor Fiona Wood and the Western Australian Burns Service, Professor Lindy Fitzgerald at Curtin University, and Professor Adina Michael-Titus at Queen Mary University of London.
