Neurodegenerative diseases including Alzheimer's disease (AD) and Parkinson's disease (PD) represent a major and growing public health burden. A central challenge in both conditions is that pathological changes begin years or decades before clinical symptoms emerge, and the biological mechanisms linking genetic susceptibility to disease onset remain incompletely understood. My research in this area applies advanced metabolic and lipidomic phenotyping to investigate the biochemical consequences of genetic risk and early disease, with the goal of identifying modifiable metabolic pathways that could serve as targets for early detection and intervention.

**Alzheimer's Disease**

A central strand of this work concerns the ABCA7 gene, one of the most significant genetic risk loci for late-onset AD. Contributing metabolic phenotyping expertise to a metabolome-wide association study published in PNAS (2022), I helped demonstrate that ABCA7 risk variants are associated with altered ceramide and lactosylceramide metabolism in peripheral blood, identifying a potentially modifiable lipid metabolic pathway linking genetic risk to neurodegeneration. This finding forms the foundation of my current Dementia Australia Royce Simmons Foundation Mid-Career Research Fellowship (2024–2026), which extends this work longitudinally within the Busselton Healthy Ageing Study cohort using targeted lipidomic profiling.

Earlier work established foundational evidence of altered phosphatidylcholine metabolism in AD (Neurobiology of Aging, 2014) and identified long-chain cholesteryl esters as lipid species associated with disease risk (Translational Psychiatry, 2015). Complementary first-author work in Alzheimer's Research and Therapy (2021) applied targeted metabolic phenotyping to a large multi-centre clinical cohort, demonstrating significant reductions in serotonin and kynurenine pathway metabolite bioavailability in both urine and serum of individuals living with AD, implicating systemic tryptophan metabolic disruption as a feature of established disease.

**Parkinson's Disease**

In Parkinson's disease, my research investigates the relationship between gastrointestinal dysfunction, immune dysregulation, and metabolic perturbation. Collaborative work published in npj Parkinson's Disease (2025) identified distinct immune and metabolic signatures characterising constipation-driven endophenotypes within the PD population, supporting the view that gut function is a biologically meaningful stratification variable in PD research. This work connects directly to the host-microbiome co-metabolism programme, where tryptophan and indole metabolism provide a mechanistic framework linking microbial metabolism to neurological outcomes.

Across both conditions, the overarching aim is to move from population-level metabolic associations towards mechanistic understanding — using integrated metabolic and genetic data to identify the pathways through which genetic risk translates into biochemical dysregulation and ultimately disease.
