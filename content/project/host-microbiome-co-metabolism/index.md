The gut microbiome produces a diverse array of neuroactive and immunomodulatory metabolites — including tryptophan-derived indoles, short-chain fatty acids, and bile acid metabolites — that can profoundly influence host physiology, brain function, and disease risk. Understanding the metabolic language of this host-microbiome interface represents one of the most exciting frontiers in biomedical research, with implications spanning neurodegeneration, mental health, metabolic disease, and healthy ageing.

**Tryptophan and Indole Metabolism**

The tryptophan metabolic pathway sits at the heart of host-microbiome co-metabolic signalling. Gut microbes compete with the host for dietary tryptophan, catabolising it to produce indole and a range of indole derivatives that act as ligands for the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) and other host receptors. My research has contributed key insights into both the bioanalytical measurement and the biological consequences of this pathway.

A landmark study published in PNAS (2021), for which I developed the tryptophan bioanalytical pipeline, demonstrated that tryptophan-metabolising gut microbes regulate adult hippocampal neurogenesis via the AhR, directly linking microbial tryptophan catabolism to brain plasticity. Subsequent work published in Microorganisms (2024) extended these findings, demonstrating that microbe-derived indoles serve as key regulators of organ growth and systemic metabolic function — indole-deficient mice showed multiorgan growth retardation, impaired gut motility, and altered TCA cycle and lipid metabolism, with a striking increase in serum melatonin consistent with oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction.

My first-author UHPLC-MS/MS assay for tryptophan pathway metabolites, published in Analytical Chemistry (2019) and incorporated into the International Phenome Centre assay portfolio, provides the bioanalytical foundation for this research programme. This validated platform enables simultaneous quantification of tryptophan, kynurenine, kynurenic acid, quinolinic acid, serotonin, and markers of gut integrity across large epidemiological and clinical sample sets.

**Host-Microbiome Co-Metabolism Across Disease Contexts**

Beyond neurogenesis, the tryptophan-indole axis has been implicated in a range of pathological contexts. Reduced tryptophan pathway metabolite bioavailability has been identified in Alzheimer's disease (Alzheimer's Research and Therapy, 2021), and kynurenine pathway activation has been characterised as a central metabolic mediator of immune-metabolic crosstalk in acute SARS-CoV-2 infection (Journal of Proteome Research, 2021). Collaborative work has also examined the impact of bariatric surgery (Scientific Reports, 2022) and antibiotic exposure (Journal of Chromatography B, 2021) on tryptophan pathway metabolism, illustrating the sensitivity of this pathway to surgical and pharmacological perturbation of the gut microbiome.

**Future Directions**

This programme continues to develop the mechanistic framework linking microbial tryptophan and indole metabolism to host neurological and metabolic outcomes, extending validated bioanalytical assays into population-scale epidemiological cohorts to investigate associations between gut-derived metabolites, cognitive function, and neurodegenerative risk. Active international collaborations with the National Neuroscience Institute of Singapore and the Australian National Phenome Centre support both mechanistic and population-scale investigations.
